The integration of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) into education
presents a paradox: the more helpful the tool, the more it risks eroding the
cognitive processes essential for deep learning. Educational research has long
shown that meaningful learning depends not only on correct answers, but
also on productive struggle, schema construction, and forms of scaffolding
that preserve rather than bypass learner effort. This challenge is especially
acute for novice learners, whose understanding is still developing and for
whom premature answer delivery may replace the generative processes
through which durable knowledge is formed. This paper introduces the
concept of the “Safety Gap”—the divergence between a student’s AI-assisted
performance and their internal capability to verify that output, leading to
potential epistemic risks when the tool fails. While GenAI offers
unprecedented opportunities for personalized support and content
generation, current Oracle-style models often eliminate the productive
struggle necessary for schema construction. Drawing on recent literature, we
argue that educational AI should be designed not merely to maximize
convenience or answer completion, but to support cognitive engagement and
independent judgment. We therefore propose a pedagogical framework
centered on Socratic and Adversarial AI architectures that withhold direct
solutions, introduce constructive cognitive friction, and prioritize processbased assessment over final outputs.

1 Introduction: the paradox of the helpful machine
The integration of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) into educational settings
is not merely a technological upgrade; it represents a fundamental restructuring of the
cognitive contract between learner and knowledge. From our perspective in medical
education, where clinical competency is non-negotiable, this shift presents a critical
juncture. On one side lies the promise of the super-powered student, augmented by an
omnipresent tutor capable of synthesizing vast ontologies in milliseconds. Conversely,
this integration introduces a subtle challenge often obscured in standard metrics: the
Safety Gap.
We define the Safety Gap as the widening chasm between the surface-level
competence a student displays when supported by helpful AI tools and their actual,
unassisted cognitive capability (Gerlich, 2025). Here, unassisted capability refers to the

often eliminate productive struggle, the very process necessary

2.2 Automation bias and the “fluency
heuristic”
The risk is compounded by automation bias, the tendency to
favor output generated by automated systems. LLMs speak with
absolute confidence and linguistic fluency, triggering a heuristic
in the human brain: “If it sounds this coherent, it must be true”
(Gerlich, 2025).
In high-stakes fields like medicine or engineering, this is
critical. A student may intellectually know an AI can
hallucinate, but cognitively, they often treat it as an objective
Oracle. This leads to a surrender of epistemic agency, where the
learner stops feeling responsible for verifying the truth of the
output. If a generation of learners loses this agency, we risk an
epistemic collapse where knowledge becomes a recursive loop of
AI-generated content unchecked by human critical thought.

3 The architectural landscape: from
oracles to tutors

To understand the danger of the Safety Gap, we must examine
how frictionless GenAI interacts with the cognitive architecture
of learning.

Not all AI is created equal. The extent of the Safety Gap
depends heavily on the specific design and personality of the
educational software. Here, “Oracle” is used as our own analytic
label for answer-delivery systems optimized for immediacy,
rather than as terminology taken from any single cited source.
We categorize the current landscape into three architectures:

2.1 Cognitive offloading and schema
construction
Learning involves the construction of schemas, mental
structures that organize knowledge and support transfer. In
educational terms, this process depends not only on exposure to
correct answers, but also on productive struggle through which
learners test, revise, and consolidate understanding. For novice
learners in particular, schema formation depends on engaging in
the generative work required to interpret, connect, and apply
new ideas. Struggle with a complex concept is therefore not
merely an obstacle to learning, but part of the process through
which knowledge structures are formed and reorganized. By
contrast, when an LLM provides an immediate polished
explanation, the learner may evaluate the output without fully
engaging in the processes required for durable understanding.

demands, it risks bypassing the desirable difficulties necessary

Educational settings should preserve some AI-free spaces to
ensure that baseline human capability can still be observed,
practiced, and assessed. Just as physical dissection is required to
understand anatomy, mental dissection—unassisted reasoning
sessions—must be preserved. Rus and Kendeou (2025) advocate
for an inverted scaffolding approach: beginning with highly
restricted access that prioritizes learner effort, and progressively
increasing access to AI tools only as learners demonstrate
domain mastery and self-regulation (Rus and Kendeou, 2025).
For example, in a clinical reasoning session, students could be
required to submit an initial differential diagnosis without AI
assistance before being allowed to consult an AI tool for
comparison and revision.

3.3 The adversarial agent
An experimental category where the AI is programmed to play
Devil’s Advocate, critiquing the student’s work or presenting
plausible errors (“lures”) that the student must identify. This
forces the student into a defensive posture, requiring highgermane load critical thinking to win the argument (Jose et al.,
2025b). A comparison of these architectural approaches and

To ensure these tools support rather than hinder
learning, future educational use should prioritize
pedagogically aligned deployments rather than unrestricted
answer-delivery systems. Rather than simply training
models on vast web corpora, developers and educators
must adapt models using Retrieval-Augmented Generation
(RAG) or pedagogically aligned system prompts that
prioritize desirable difficulties.
The Safety Gap is not a reason to reject the technology, but a
mandate to master it. The true value of a human in the age of AI
lies not in retrieving facts—the machine wins that race—but in
metacognition, empathy, and epistemic agency (Estaphan et al.,
2025). It is our duty to design systems that challenge, confound,
and stretch students, ensuring that learners retain independent
judgment, metacognitive awareness, and the capacity to verify
AI-assisted outputs.

4.3 Require: process-based assessment
In the age of GenAI, grading the final product (the essay or the
code) is insufficient. We must shift to Process-Based Assessment.
Recognizing that reviewing every interaction log is not scalable,
we advocate for assessing metacognitive reflection. Students
should be required to submit a brief rationale explaining why
they accepted or revised the AI’s suggestions, thereby evidencing
their critical engagement. A passing grade should require
evidence that the student critically evaluated the AI’s
suggestions, justified revisions, and verified the final output,
rather than blindly accepting the machine’s response (Thesen
and Park, 2025). In practice, this could take the form of a short
reflection in which students explain one AI suggestion they
accepted, one they rejected, and how they verified the final
answer (Zimmerman, 2002).

4.4 Refine: adversarial learning

Funding

GenAI can also be used to create opportunities for
productive failure and critical evaluation. Instead of artificially
engineering misinformation, educators should leverage the
inherent limitations of current LLMs—such as cultural biases,
hallucinations in complex medical cases, or confident but
incorrect citations—as organic adversarial examples for
students to critique (Golchini et al., 2025). Finding and
refuting AI hallucinations turns the tool’s weakness into a
critical thinking exercise, transforming the interaction from
passive consumption to active evaluation. For example,
students could be asked to identify a plausible but
unsupported claim in an AI-generated explanation and then
revise the answer using verified sources.
